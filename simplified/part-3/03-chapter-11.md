## Justice and Natural Rights

<p id="3-11-1" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-1" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.1"><span className="verse-num">3.11.1</span></a> The feeling of justice is natural in human beings. Education can develop it, but it does not create it. Even a simple person may sometimes judge more justly than someone with more learning.</p>

<p id="3-11-2" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-2" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.2"><span className="verse-num">3.11.2</span></a> When people disagree about justice, passion often distorts their judgment. Self-interest, pride, and fear make them see wrongly. In its true sense, justice is respect for the rights of others.</p>

### Human Law and Natural Law

<p id="3-11-3" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-3" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.3"><span className="verse-num">3.11.3</span></a> We recognize the rights of others through human law and natural law.</p>

<p id="3-11-4" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-4" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.4"><span className="verse-num">3.11.4</span></a> Human laws change with time, place, and the progress of society. They are useful for social order, but they are limited and can be imperfect. Natural law is higher. It expresses what is just in itself, and conscience often sees this even when written law fails.</p>

### The Measure of True Justice

<p id="3-11-5" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-5" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.5"><span className="verse-num">3.11.5</span></a> The clearest rule of justice is this: do to others what you would want others to do to you.</p>

<p id="3-11-6" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-6" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.6"><span className="verse-num">3.11.6</span></a> When in doubt, a person should ask what they would judge fair if places were exchanged. But this rule must be applied sincerely, not twisted by selfishness. True justice begins when a person respects the rights of others as much as their own.</p>

### Justice in Social Life

<p id="3-11-7" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-7" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.7"><span className="verse-num">3.11.7</span></a> Life in society brings both rights and duties. The first duty is to respect the rights of others. Whoever does this is just.</p>

<p id="3-11-8" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-8" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.8"><span className="verse-num">3.11.8</span></a> When justice is violated, peace is broken. People then try to recover by force what they think was denied to them. To understand the limits of our own rights, we should allow others the same rights we claim for ourselves.</p>

### Equality, Authority, and Subordination

<p id="3-11-9" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-9" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.9"><span className="verse-num">3.11.9</span></a> Natural rights are the same for all. Before God, all are equal, and no one is greater by nature than another. Social positions may differ, but these do not destroy that basic equality.</p>

<p id="3-11-10" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-10" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.10"><span className="verse-num">3.11.10</span></a> Still, equality does not remove order, authority, or subordination. People naturally recognize real superiority in wisdom, virtue, and ability. Lasting authority rests on moral worth and intelligence, not on rank alone.</p>

### Justice in Its Full Purity

<p id="3-11-11" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-11" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.11"><span className="verse-num">3.11.11</span></a> Justice in its full purity is not only the strict respect of rights. It is united with charity and love of neighbor.</p>

<p id="3-11-12" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-12" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.12"><span className="verse-num">3.11.12</span></a> Without charity, justice is incomplete. True justice respects what is due to each person, but it also includes kindness, mercy, and a real desire to do good to others.</p>

## The Right of Ownership. Theft

<p id="3-11-13" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-13" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.13"><span className="verse-num">3.11.13</span></a> The right to live is one of the first natural rights. Because of that, each person also has the right to seek what is needed to live and to put something aside for times of need.</p>

<p id="3-11-14" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-14" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.14"><span className="verse-num">3.11.14</span></a> What a person gains by honest work is legitimate property. Since it is the fruit of labor, it should be respected. The wish to possess is natural, but it must have a right purpose. To acquire what is needed for independence and security is lawful. To seek wealth only for selfish enjoyment or endless accumulation is abuse.</p>

<p id="3-11-15" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-15" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.15"><span className="verse-num">3.11.15</span></a> Property agrees with moral law when it is honestly earned and rightly used. What we possess should never make us forget charity or the needs of others.</p>

### Legitimate Ownership

<p id="3-11-16" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-16" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.16"><span className="verse-num">3.11.16</span></a> Only what is acquired without injury to others is fully legitimate.</p>

<p id="3-11-17" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-17" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.17"><span className="verse-num">3.11.17</span></a> Legal possession alone is not enough. If something has been gained through violence, fraud, exploitation, or any unfair means, it is not truly justly owned, even if human law allows it. The rule is the same as in all moral life: do not do to others what you would not want done to yourself.</p>

<p id="3-11-18" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-18" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.18"><span className="verse-num">3.11.18</span></a> Anything taken against justice is a form of theft, whatever name people give it.</p>

### The Limits of Ownership

<p id="3-11-19" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-19" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.19"><span className="verse-num">3.11.19</span></a> The right of ownership exists, but it has limits.</p>

<p id="3-11-20" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-20" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.20"><span className="verse-num">3.11.20</span></a> What is honestly acquired belongs to its owner, yet human laws are imperfect and do not always match true justice. A thing may be legal and still not be right.</p>

<p id="3-11-21" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-21" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.21"><span className="verse-num">3.11.21</span></a> Real justice rests on fairness, love, and respect for the rights of others. Ownership is rightful when it comes from honest labor, harms no one, and is used without forgetting our duty toward our neighbor.</p>

## Charity and Love for Our Neighbor

<p id="3-11-22" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-22" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.22"><span className="verse-num">3.11.22</span></a> Charity means kindness toward everyone, patience with the faults of others, and forgiveness for wrongs.</p>

<p id="3-11-23" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-23" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.23"><span className="verse-num">3.11.23</span></a> Love and charity complete the law of justice. To love our neighbor is to do for others the good we would want for ourselves, in all our relationships.</p>

<p id="3-11-24" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-24" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.24"><span className="verse-num">3.11.24</span></a> True charity does not honor the rich and ignore the poor. It treats those who suffer with respect and helps restore their dignity.</p>

### Loving One’s Enemies

<p id="3-11-25" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-25" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.25"><span className="verse-num">3.11.25</span></a> To love one’s enemies does not mean feeling affection for them. It means forgiving them and returning good for evil.</p>

<p id="3-11-26" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-26" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.26"><span className="verse-num">3.11.26</span></a> By doing this, a person rises above hatred instead of being ruled by it. The real test of charity is to choose kindness even when we have been hurt.</p>

### Alms-Giving and True Benevolence

<p id="3-11-27" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-27" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.27"><span className="verse-num">3.11.27</span></a> Begging often degrades a person. A just society should provide for those who cannot work, without humiliation.</p>

<p id="3-11-28" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-28" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.28"><span className="verse-num">3.11.28</span></a> Giving alms is not wrong, but the spirit in which it is given matters greatly. True charity does not always wait to be asked; it looks for those in need.</p>

<p id="3-11-29" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-29" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.29"><span className="verse-num">3.11.29</span></a> A gift offered with gentleness does more than meet a need, while pride or harshness wounds. Many suffer quietly, and wise people learn to notice this hidden poverty and help without display.</p>

### The Law of Love

<p id="3-11-30" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-30" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.30"><span className="verse-num">3.11.30</span></a> Love one another: this is the whole law. It is the divine principle that governs moral life.</p>

<p id="3-11-31" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-31" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.31"><span className="verse-num">3.11.31</span></a> No spirit is completely alone. Each one receives guidance from those more advanced and owes care and patience to those less advanced.</p>

<p id="3-11-32" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-32" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.32"><span className="verse-num">3.11.32</span></a> So charity is much more than occasional giving. It means seeking hidden suffering, bearing with faults, teaching the ignorant, and helping those who have fallen instead of despising them.</p>

### Responsibility and Moral Education

<p id="3-11-33" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-33" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.33"><span className="verse-num">3.11.33</span></a> Some people do fall into misery through their own faults. Even so, harsh judgment is too easy. If they had been given sound moral education, many might have avoided the habits that ruined them.</p>

<p id="3-11-34" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-34" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.34"><span className="verse-num">3.11.34</span></a> Much social suffering can be prevented before it has to be relieved. The world improves when people are taught duty, self-control, justice, and charity.</p>

## Maternal and Filial Love

<p id="3-11-35" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-35" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.35"><span className="verse-num">3.11.35</span></a> A mother’s love is a law of nature that protects the child and preserves life. In human beings, it can rise above instinct and become a true virtue.</p>

<p id="3-11-36" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-36" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.36"><span className="verse-num">3.11.36</span></a> In animals, this attachment usually lasts only while the young need care. In people, it may last throughout life and show itself in devotion, sacrifice, and self-denial. Filial love has the same character. It is not only gratitude, but a bond strengthened by mutual affection and one of the purest ties in family life.</p>

### Mothers Who Do Not Love Their Children

<p id="3-11-37" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-37" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.37"><span className="verse-num">3.11.37</span></a> When a mother does not love her child, something is out of order in a bond that should be natural. This coldness may be a trial for the child or the result of faults from the past.</p>

<p id="3-11-38" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-38" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.38"><span className="verse-num">3.11.38</span></a> Still, the mother is not excused. Her lack of affection shows moral weakness because she fails to support, protect, and guide. The child, by bearing this suffering with patience, may gain moral strength.</p>

### The Duty of Parents Toward Difficult Children

<p id="3-11-39" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-39" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.39"><span className="verse-num">3.11.39</span></a> Parents are not freed from duty because a child is difficult or causes sorrow. They must still love, instruct, correct, and do all they can to turn the child toward the good.</p>

<p id="3-11-40" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-40" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.40"><span className="verse-num">3.11.40</span></a> This duty requires patience and perseverance. Sometimes parents suffer in part because they allowed faults to grow early, and may reap what they have sown.</p>

<p id="3-11-41" className="verse"><a href="#3-11-41" className="verse-link" aria-label="Link to verse 3.11.41"><span className="verse-num">3.11.41</span></a> Family love is not only a feeling. It is also a duty of constancy, sacrifice, and responsibility, especially when tested by pain.</p>
