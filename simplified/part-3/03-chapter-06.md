## Necessary Destruction and Abusive Destruction

<p id="3-6-1" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.1</span> Destruction is part of nature, but only in a limited sense. What seems destroyed is often only transformed, so life can be renewed and progress continue.</p>

<p id="3-6-2" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.2</span> Living beings destroy one another in order to live, helping maintain balance in nature. Only the outer form is destroyed; the intelligent principle is not. It survives these changes and continues developing.</p>

<p id="3-6-3" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.3</span> So preservation and destruction work together in the order of life.</p>

### Self-Preservation and the Proper Time of Death

<p id="3-6-4" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.4</span> Even though destruction is necessary, every being has the instinct to preserve itself. Death should come at its proper time, not before.</p>

<p id="3-6-5" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.5</span> If life ends too soon, the development of the intelligent principle is interrupted. For this reason, beings are driven to protect life and continue their species.</p>

<p id="3-6-6" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.6</span> Fear of death serves the same purpose. Though death may free the soul, that fear helps keep a person in earthly life until the needed work is done.</p>

### Destruction According to the State of Worlds

<p id="3-6-7" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.7</span> The need for destruction is not the same in every world. It depends on how material the world is.</p>

<p id="3-6-8" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.8</span> The more material a world is, the more destruction belongs to its condition. As worlds become more purified, physically and morally, destruction becomes less necessary.</p>

<p id="3-6-9" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.9</span> The same is true on earth. As human beings advance, they feel more repugnance toward useless destruction.</p>

### The Limits of Human Destruction

<p id="3-6-10" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.10</span> In humanity’s present state, people do not have an unlimited right to destroy animals. That right extends only as far as necessity, such as nourishment and protection.</p>

<p id="3-6-11" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.11</span> Beyond necessity, destruction becomes abuse. To kill only for the pleasure of killing shows moral inferiority.</p>

<p id="3-6-12" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.12</span> Animals destroy from need. Human beings can go beyond need, and are responsible for that.</p>

### False Scruples and True Humaneness

<p id="3-6-13" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.13</span> Being overly scrupulous about killing animals is not, by itself, a sign of real superiority.</p>

<p id="3-6-14" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.14</span> The desire to avoid causing suffering is good. But if it becomes excessive while more serious wrongs are ignored, it loses much of its worth.</p>

<p id="3-6-15" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.15</span> What matters is a right use of life, governed by necessity, compassion, and moral duty.</p>

## Destructive Calamities

<p id="3-6-16" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.16</span> Destructive calamities have a place in human progress.</p>

<p id="3-6-17" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.17</span> They can hasten change and help bring moral renewal. This does not mean people must suffer in order to improve. Human beings already know enough to choose between good and evil. But when they misuse their freedom, suffering can become a correction. It breaks pride and reminds people how fragile they are.</p>

<p id="3-6-18" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.18</span> A painful question remains: why do good people suffer along with the guilty? If we look only at earthly life, this seems unjust. But the body is temporary, while the spirit lives on. Those who die in great disasters are not lost. In the end, death by calamity is not different in nature from death by any other cause, though it may come more suddenly.</p>

### Physical Usefulness

<p id="3-6-19" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.19</span> Calamities also have physical usefulness.</p>

<p id="3-6-20" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.20</span> They can reshape the land and change the conditions of life. Often the benefit is not for those who suffer through them, but for those who come afterward. What seems only destructive now may prepare the way for better conditions later.</p>

### Moral Trials

<p id="3-6-21" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.21</span> Calamities are also moral trials.</p>

<p id="3-6-22" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.22</span> In times of hardship, people may show courage, patience, resignation, self-denial, detachment from material things, and love of neighbor. Trials uncover what is really in the heart. If selfishness rules, the suffering teaches little. If humility and charity prevail, it can aid moral progress.</p>

### May We Avert the Calamities That Afflict Us?

<p id="3-6-23" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.23</span> Many calamities can be prevented, at least in part.</p>

<p id="3-6-24" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.24</span> This is done not by superstition, but by knowledge and foresight. Much suffering comes from human neglect. As people better understand natural causes, they can avoid many dangers or lessen their effects.</p>

<p id="3-6-25" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.25</span> Still, not every affliction can be escaped. Some belong to a wider providential order and must be met with resignation to the will of God. Even so, human carelessness often makes disasters worse. Science, agriculture, engineering, and hygiene can prevent much suffering or reduce its force.</p>

<p id="3-6-26" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.26</span> Material well-being grows when intelligence is rightly used, but intelligence alone is not enough. Charity is also needed. When knowledge and charity work together, much suffering can be avoided or softened.</p>

## War

<p id="3-6-27" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.27</span> War comes from the dominance of our lower, animal nature over our spiritual nature. It is born from violent passions and the desire to dominate.</p>

<p id="3-6-28" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.28</span> Among barbaric peoples, where the law of the strongest prevails, war seems natural. As humanity advances, it becomes less frequent, because its causes are better understood and more often prevented. It will disappear when people live by justice and God's law, and nations see one another as brothers and sisters.</p>

### Freedom and Progress

<p id="3-6-29" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.29</span> Providence may allow war to produce results that aid freedom and progress.</p>

<p id="3-6-30" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.30</span> This does not make war good or conquest honorable. Human passions are still its true cause, but a higher wisdom may bring some good from that evil.</p>

### The Guilt of Those Who Provoke War

<p id="3-6-31" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.31</span> Those who provoke war for their own profit bear great guilt.</p>

<p id="3-6-32" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.32</span> Their fault is grave, because they seek bloodshed through ambition, pride, or self-interest. They are accountable for the lives lost and must repair the moral harm caused by the deaths for which they are responsible.</p>

## Murder

<p id="3-6-33" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.33</span> Taking another person’s life is a grave wrong against divine order. The life destroyed may have been meant for atonement, repair, learning, or a mission. Yet guilt is not judged by the outward act alone: intention, motive, and inner state are also considered.</p>

### Legitimate Defense

<p id="3-6-34" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.34</span> Only necessity can excuse killing in self-defense. If a person can preserve their own life by escape, restraint, or some other means without taking the aggressor’s life, that should be done. Taking life is excused only when no other real way exists.</p>

### Murder in War

<p id="3-6-35" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.35</span> Those who kill in war under force are not judged the same as those who kill from personal hatred. Coercion lessens responsibility but does not remove it. Cruelty remains blameworthy, while mercy and humanity are taken into account.</p>

### Parricide and Infanticide

<p id="3-6-36" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.36</span> No murder becomes less serious because of the victim’s age or family relationship. Parricide and infanticide are equally guilty. Before divine justice, the essential wrong is the violation of life.</p>

### Infanticide in Intellectually Advanced Societies

<p id="3-6-37" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.37</span> A people may advance in intelligence without advancing in morality. That is why infanticide and other cruel customs can exist in societies that seem advanced. Intellectual progress does not purify the conscience, and knowledge alone does not make anyone good.</p>

## Cruelty

<p id="3-6-38" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.38</span> Cruelty is connected to the instinct of destruction, but it is its worst form. Destruction may sometimes have a place in the order of things; cruelty never does. It always shows an evil nature.</p>

<p id="3-6-39" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.39</span> Among primitive peoples, physical life weighs more heavily than spiritual life. Because they are focused on material needs and self-preservation, they are more open to cruelty. Imperfect spirits also strengthen these tendencies until more advanced peoples lessen their influence.</p>

<p id="3-6-40" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.40</span> Cruelty does not come from a total lack of moral sense, because moral sense exists in everyone, though in different degrees. In primitive people it may lie dormant, but with growth it becomes goodness, compassion, and humanity. When material instincts are overstimulated, they smother the moral sense. As moral progress increases, the animal side loses its power.</p>

<p id="3-6-41" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.41</span> So even in civilized societies, some people can still be as cruel as barbarians. Spirits of a lower order may be born among more advanced peoples in order to progress, but if the trial is too hard, their old instincts can return.</p>

<p id="3-6-42" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.42</span> Still, humanity moves forward. Those dominated by evil, and unfit for more moral societies, will little by little disappear from them. In new lives and through new trials, they will learn the difference between good and evil. Progress is slow, but certain, and cruelty fades as the moral sense awakens.</p>

## Dueling

<p id="3-6-43" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.43</span> Dueling is not lawful self-defense. It is murder preserved by a senseless custom, unworthy of a morally advanced society. If a person enters a duel knowing they are likely to die, it is also suicide. If the chances are equal, it remains both murder and suicide.</p>

<p id="3-6-44" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.44</span> In every case, the dueler is responsible for trying to kill another and for risking their own life for no real good.</p>

### The So-Called Point of Honor

<p id="3-6-45" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.45</span> What is called the “point of honor” is usually pride and vanity. People may imagine that honor requires a duel or that refusing one is cowardice, but true honor is above violent passion.</p>

<p id="3-6-46" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.46</span> A wrong is not repaired by killing someone or by being killed. Real honor lies in admitting a fault when one is wrong, forgiving when one is right, and refusing to treat harmless insults as important.</p>

## The Death Penalty

<p id="3-6-47" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.47</span> The death penalty will disappear from human law.</p>

<p id="3-6-48" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.48</span> Its decline marks moral progress. As people become more enlightened, they see more clearly that no one should claim the final right over another’s life. This is already visible where society has become more humane.</p>

<p id="3-6-49" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.49</span> Even now, progress appears in the restriction of capital punishment. Fewer crimes are punished by death, the accused receive greater legal protection, and the condemned are treated less cruelly than in earlier times.</p>

### Self-Preservation and Society

<p id="3-6-50" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.50</span> The right of self-preservation does not justify killing a dangerous member of society when society can be protected in other ways.</p>

<p id="3-6-51" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.51</span> A community has the right to defend itself, but if safer and more just means exist, they should be used. To kill the offender is also to remove the chance for repentance and moral renewal.</p>

### Was It Ever Necessary?

<p id="3-6-52" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.52</span> What people call necessary is often only what they have not yet learned to replace.</p>

<p id="3-6-53" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.53</span> In less developed times, harsh practices seemed unavoidable because no better remedy was known. As societies become more enlightened, they reject acts once committed in the name of justice during ignorance.</p>

### Civilization and the Restriction of Capital Punishment

<p id="3-6-54" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.54</span> The shrinking number of cases in which the death penalty is used is a sign of civilization’s progress.</p>

<p id="3-6-55" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.55</span> History contains many judicial killings once treated as righteous. What one age accepts as normal, another later sees as barbaric. This shows that human laws change as humanity advances, while divine law alone is eternal.</p>

### The Meaning of “Whoever Kills by the Sword”

<p id="3-6-56" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.56</span> The words, “Whoever kills by the sword shall perish by the sword,” do not give human beings the right to take revenge or claim an absolute right over a murderer’s life.</p>

<p id="3-6-57" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.57</span> True retribution belongs to divine justice. Wrongdoing brings consequences under a higher law. This saying means moral consequence, not permission for human killing.</p>

<p id="3-6-58" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.58</span> It must also be understood with the command to forgive one’s enemies. Justice without mercy distorts that teaching.</p>

### The Death Penalty in the Name of God

<p id="3-6-59" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.59</span> To impose the death penalty in the name of God is to claim an authority that belongs only to divine justice.</p>

<p id="3-6-60" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.60</span> It falsely turns human severity into something sacred. No one can make execution holy by saying it is God’s will.</p>

### Justice, Progress, and Mercy

<p id="3-6-61" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.61</span> Humanity advances by leaving cruelty behind and moving toward laws shaped by respect for life and the possibility of repentance.</p>

<p id="3-6-62" className="verse"><span className="verse-num">3.6.62</span> The death penalty belongs to a less enlightened stage of society. As understanding grows, justice becomes less violent and more in harmony with divine law. The future of civilized society points to its complete abolition.</p>
